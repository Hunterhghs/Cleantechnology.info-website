FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to common questions about clean technology and the energy transition.

What exactly is "clean technology"? +

Clean technology (cleantech) refers to any product, service, or process that uses renewable materials and energy sources, dramatically reduces the use of natural resources, and cuts or eliminates emissions and waste. It spans energy, transportation, agriculture, water, manufacturing, and construction sectors. The goal is to deliver superior performance while minimizing environmental impact.

Is renewable energy really cheaper than fossil fuels now? +

Yes, in most of the world. According to the IEA and IRENA, new solar PV and onshore wind are now the cheapest sources of new electricity generation in countries representing over 90% of global power generation. Solar module costs have dropped over 90% since 2010, and the levelized cost of energy (LCOE) for utility-scale solar is often $20–$50 per MWh — well below new coal or natural gas plants. However, system costs including storage and grid integration are important additional factors.

Can we really power everything with clean energy? +

The technical potential of renewable energy far exceeds global energy demand. Major studies from Stanford University, the IEA, and others have laid out detailed pathways to 100% clean energy systems. The challenges are primarily in storage (for intermittency), industrial heat, and long-distance transportation — areas where hydrogen, advanced batteries, and sustainable fuels are rapidly improving. Most credible scenarios see clean energy supplying 80–100% of electricity by 2050.

What about the environmental cost of making solar panels and batteries? +

Manufacturing solar panels and batteries does require energy and materials, and their production has an environmental footprint. However, lifecycle analyses consistently show that renewable energy technologies produce far less CO₂ per kWh over their lifetime than fossil fuel alternatives — typically 10–50× less. A solar panel "pays back" its manufacturing energy within 1–3 years, then delivers 25–30 years of clean electricity. Battery recycling programs are also scaling rapidly to address end-of-life impacts.

How can I personally support the clean technology transition? +

There are many ways to contribute: switching to a renewable energy provider, considering an EV or hybrid for your next vehicle, improving your home's energy efficiency, supporting companies and policies that advance clean technology, investing in clean energy funds, and simply staying informed and sharing knowledge. Every decision that shifts demand toward cleaner options sends a market signal that accelerates the transition.

What role does government policy play in clean tech adoption? +

Policy is a critical accelerator. Carbon pricing, renewable portfolio standards, tax credits (like the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act), feed-in tariffs, and emissions regulations have all proven effective at driving clean technology deployment. The IEA estimates that over 70% of global clean energy investment is directly influenced by policy frameworks. International agreements like the Paris Climate Agreement set overarching targets that guide national action.
